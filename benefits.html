SDG 3 - Good Health & Wellbeing

Benefits of Green Spaces

What time in nature actually does for you

Why Nature Is Good for You

Getting outside into a park or garden is one of the easiest things you can do to feel better. It doesn't cost anything, and the effects are surprisingly powerful.

More Than Just Fresh Air

Green spaces do real work for public health. The WHO's review of green and blue spaces found that people living near parks tend to have lower rates of anxiety and depression. They're also more likely to be physically active and to actually know their neighbours.

The UN picked up on this too. SDG 3 is about making sure people can live healthy lives at any age. Having a green space nearby is a big part of that, especially in cities where it's all concrete and traffic.

The "nature pill": You don't need to go on a massive hike. Just 20 to 30 minutes sitting or walking in a green space is enough to noticeably drop your cortisol levels - that's according to a study published in Frontiers in Psychology. Cortisol is the hormone your body produces when you're stressed.

Four Key Benefits

Here's what the research points to as the main ways green spaces help us out.

Mental Health & Stress Reduction

Being around trees and plants lowers cortisol, brings down blood pressure and just puts you in a better mood. People who visit parks regularly report less anxiety. There's even evidence that just looking at greenery through a hospital window helps patients recover faster - a classic 1984 study by Roger Ulrich found that patients with a tree view had shorter stays and needed fewer painkillers.

Physical Fitness & Heart Health

Parks give you somewhere to walk, run, cycle or play a game of football. People who've got a green space nearby are about 25% more likely to hit the recommended weekly activity levels, according to a WHO Europe review. That means a lower risk of heart disease, obesity and type 2 diabetes down the line.

Social Connection & Community

Parks are where people actually meet each other. Whether it's a parkrun on a Saturday morning, a community garden, or just sitting on a bench chatting, these spaces get neighbours talking. That matters a lot for older people and anyone who's just moved to a new area and doesn't know anyone yet.

Clean Air & Environmental Health

Trees filter out pollutants and soak up CO&sub2;. They cool down built-up areas, cut down on noise, and give bees and butterflies somewhere to live. Better air quality means fewer people ending up in hospital with asthma and other breathing problems.

Who Benefits Most?

Green spaces are good for everyone, but some groups feel the effects more than others.

Children

Kids who play outside develop better motor skills and stronger immune systems. There's also evidence that children with regular access to nature concentrate better in school and show fewer symptoms of ADHD - a study in the American Journal of Public Health found that green outdoor settings reduced ADHD symptoms in children.

Older Adults

A nearby park gives older people somewhere to get a bit of gentle exercise and have a chat. That helps prevent the isolation and loneliness that can really hit people in later life, and it keeps them moving too.

Urban Communities

If you live in a densely built area, a park is your escape from noise and traffic. It cleans the air for the whole neighbourhood and gives you somewhere free to go that isn't your flat or a shop.

“Nature is not a place to visit. It is home.” - Gary Snyder, poet and environmental activist
