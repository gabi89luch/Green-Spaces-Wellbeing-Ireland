SDG 11 - Sustainable Cities & Communities

Green Spaces in Ireland

From city parks to wild coastlines, here's what we've got

Ireland's Green Space Network

Ireland has a real mix of green spaces. You've got historic parks like Phoenix Park, wild trails along the coast, and small community gardens tucked into neighbourhoods. Together they form a network that keeps our towns and cities liveable.

Building Sustainable Cities with Nature

The UN's SDG 11 is about making cities work for everyone. One of the targets in this goal is making sure people have access to green and public spaces, no matter who they are. It specifically mentions women, children, older people and people with disabilities - groups that often get left out when cities are being planned.

Ireland has done a fair bit on this front, but there's still a gap. Dublin has less green space per person than most other European capitals. Things like the Dublin City Biodiversity Action Plan and the All-Ireland Pollinator Plan are trying to fix that, but it's a work in progress.

SDG 11 Target 11.7: By 2030, everyone should have access to safe, inclusive green and public spaces. That includes women, children, older persons and people with disabilities. Read the full target on the UN site.

Featured Green Spaces

Here are some of the green spaces we think are worth knowing about.

Dublin

Phoenix Park

At 709 hectares, Phoenix Park is one of the biggest enclosed parks in any European capital, according to the OPW. It's got a herd of wild fallow deer roaming around, and over 10 million people visit it every year. If you live in Dublin, you've probably been there.

Cork

Fitzgerald's Park

Sitting on the banks of the River Lee, this 18-hectare park has gardens, a museum and nice riverside walks. It's where a lot of events in Cork happen during the summer.

Galway

NUI Galway Campus & Corrib

The riverside walks along the Corrib are some of the best urban nature trails in the west of Ireland. You'll see plenty of birdlife and native plants along the way. The university campus itself is pretty green too.

Nationwide

Community Gardens

There are over 200 community gardens in Ireland, according to Community Gardens Ireland. They turn empty patches of land into places where people grow food, learn new skills and actually talk to their neighbours. Some of the best ones started as just a few people clearing a vacant lot.

Wicklow

Powerscourt & Glendalough

The Wicklow Mountains have some of the best protected woodland and valley landscapes in the country. Glendalough's old monastic settlement sits in a glacial valley, so you get history and nature in the same place.

Dublin

Marlay & St Anne's Parks

These big suburban parks are where thousands of Dublin families go at the weekend. You've got sports pitches, woodland walks, farmers' markets and playgrounds. They're basically everyday green infrastructure for the people living nearby.

Types of Green Spaces

"Green space" covers a lot of ground (no pun intended). Here's a quick breakdown of the main types you'll find in Ireland.

  • Urban Parks - the big public parks in cities. Phoenix Park and St Stephen's Green are the obvious Dublin examples.
  • Pocket Parks - small green areas tucked into neighbourhoods. You might walk past one every day and not even think of it as a "park".
  • Community Gardens - shared spaces where local residents grow food and flowers together.
  • Greenways & Trails - long paths for walking and cycling, like the Great Western Greenway in Mayo.
  • Nature Reserves & Forests - protected areas like Killarney National Park that are a bit wilder and great for biodiversity.
  • Green Roofs & Walls - plants growing on buildings. They cool things down, soak up rain and help pollinators in built-up areas.
